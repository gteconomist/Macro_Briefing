# Daily Macro Briefing — Thursday, May 21, 2026

## Top of mind

The dominant macro story this week is the renewed surge in long-end Treasury yields. The 10-year hit 4.67% and the 30-year 5.18% as of Tuesday — both up 6 bps in a single session — while the 2s10s spread holds near 54 bps and the 10Y-3M spread has compressed slightly to 92 bps. WTI at $112.25 and Brent at $116.73 (latest available through 5/18) add a stagflationary overlay: the Fed is pinned at 3.50–3.75% while real energy costs are ratcheting up, and the FOMC minutes released yesterday will be scrutinized for any signal on how the committee is mapping that combination.

## Yesterday's releases (Wed 5/20)

- **FOMC Minutes (May meeting):** Released at 14:00. With the effective fed funds rate at 3.62% and the policy rate unchanged since the last cut, the key read is how members characterized the inflation-versus-labor-market tradeoff at this juncture — particularly given WTI above $112 and CPI index levels still elevated. Any language tightening the bar for further cuts, or flagging upside inflation risks from energy and tariff pass-through, will reinforce the bear steepening dynamic already visible in the 30-year.

Watch whether the claims trend is stabilizing or continuing to creep up, given that April UNRATE held at 4.3% and payroll gains slowed to +115K.

## Fed watch

Target range is 3.50–3.75%, with the effective rate at 3.62% and SOFR at 3.50% — both well-behaved within band. The FOMC minutes dropped yesterday afternoon; with the 30-year Treasury at 5.18% and crude above $112, the bond market is doing incremental tightening at the long end regardless of what the Fed does at the short end. No FOMC meeting, minutes, Beige Book, or SEP release appears in the remaining week-ahead calendar, so the next formal policy signal beyond the minutes is out beyond this week. The near-term watch is whether the bear steepening in 10s and 30s starts to bite housing or credit — IG OAS is still remarkably tight at 76 bps and HY at 286 bps, suggesting credit markets haven't yet priced the yield backup as a stress signal.
