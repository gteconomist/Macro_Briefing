# Daily Macro Briefing — Friday, June 12, 2026

## Top of mind

The dominant overnight driver is the reported breakthrough in Iran war talks, which catalyzed a broad risk-on move: S&P 500 closed Thursday at 7,394 (+1.75%), NASDAQ at 25,810 (+2.54%), and DJIA at 50,849 (+1.86%). Oil has been a contradictory signal here — WTI at $95.00 and Brent at $97.46 remain elevated and haven't pulled back meaningfully on the geopolitical news, which bears watching as a potential pass-through risk to inflation. Wednesday's CPI print (May headline index: 333.979, up from 332.407) and Thursday's PPI release together will shape the near-term inflation read going into the Fed's next decision window.

## Yesterday's releases (Thu 6/11)

- **PPI (May):** Index at 157.659, up from 156.011 in April (+1.056 points, roughly +0.7% m/m). That's a meaningful acceleration at the producer level and, combined with Wednesday's CPI, reinforces that tariff and energy passthrough has not fully dissipated in the pipeline.
- **Initial Jobless Claims (week ending 6/6):** 229,000, up 4,000 from the prior week's 225,000. Continuing claims (week ending 5/30) rose to 1.795M from 1.771M. Both remain within the range consistent with a cooling but not cracking labor market — the drift higher in continuing claims is the more telling signal.

## Fed watch

Target range remains 3.50–3.75%, with the effective fed funds rate at 3.62% and SOFR at 3.59% — both tracking comfortably inside the corridor. The 10-year is at 4.55% (+2 bps from Tuesday), the 30-year has crossed 5.03%, and the 2-year holds at 4.13%, leaving a 10Y–2Y spread of 42 bps. The T10Y3M spread has narrowed to 67 bps from 76 bps the prior day, worth monitoring if the long end continues to sell off. No FOMC meeting, minutes, Beige Book, or SEP release appears in the calendar for the next five business days. The policy calculus stays data-dependent: a hot Michigan inflation expectations print this morning, layered on top of this week's PPI and CPI, could push back market pricing for any near-term cut even as the geopolitical risk premium partially unwinds.
